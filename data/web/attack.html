Deauthentication Attack (Deauth)

The deauthentication attack is a Wi-Fi network disruption technique that forcibly disconnects selected client devices from a wireless access point by transmitting deauthentication frames. These frames falsely inform devices that they are no longer authorized to remain connected to the network.

This attack is effective primarily because many legacy or improperly configured devices do not implement the IEEE 802.11w-2009 standard, which introduces Management Frame Protection (MFP) to secure deauthentication and disassociation frames against spoofing.

Due to the channel-specific nature of Wi-Fi communications, only a single target should be selected at a time. Selecting multiple targets operating on different channels can cause rapid channel hopping, leading to unstable behavior and loss of connectivity to the access point hosting the control interface.

Purpose: Used for testing network resilience, identifying unsecured management frames, and demonstrating the importance of modern Wi-Fi security standards.

Beacon Frame Injection (Beacon Attack)

Beacon frames are management packets broadcast by wireless access points to announce their presence and network configuration details, such as the SSID and supported capabilities.

In a beacon injection attack, forged beacon frames are continuously transmitted to simulate the existence of multiple wireless networks. This causes nearby devices to detect and display fake access points, giving the appearance of newly created Wi-Fi networks.

Custom network names (SSIDs) can be specified to control how these simulated networks appear to nearby devices.

Purpose: Primarily used for educational demonstrations, testing wireless scanning tools, and analyzing how client devices respond to large numbers of broadcast networks.

Probe Request Injection (Probe Attack)

Probe requests are transmitted by Wi-Fi client devices to actively search for known or preferred networks. These requests may contain SSIDs of networks the device has previously connected to.

Probe request injection involves broadcasting probe requests for specified SSIDs to simulate client devices searching for those networks. This can introduce noise into wireless monitoring environments and potentially interfere with Wi-Fi tracking or analytics systems.

This technique typically has minimal visible impact on standard home networks but can be useful in controlled environments for studying client behavior and privacy implications related to wireless probing.

Purpose: Used for research, privacy analysis, and understanding how wireless devices reveal network history through probe requests.
